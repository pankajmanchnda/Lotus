# U.S. Market And Compliance Brief

This is product-planning research, not legal advice. Before launch, use qualified FDA/FTC counsel, import counsel, privacy counsel, Ayurvedic practitioners, and medical reviewers.

## Bottom Line

Ananta Ayurveda Engine is commercially viable in the United States if positioned as a **wellness education, intake, adherence, and practitioner-support platform**.

It becomes legally and safety-risky if it:

- Diagnoses diseases.
- Claims to treat, cure, mitigate, or prevent disease.
- Recommends prescription-like regimens without clinician review.
- Sells imported Ayurvedic products without rigorous contaminant testing and compliant U.S. labeling.
- Markets heavy-metal, mineral, bhasma, or rasaushadhi preparations directly to consumers.

## Safest Launch Model

1. Launch as a software and education product first.
2. Keep product commerce separate from the recommendation engine until labels, claims, suppliers, import pathways, GMP, and testing are reviewed.
3. Use structure/function-style wellness language such as "supports digestive comfort" instead of disease-treatment language.
4. Require practitioner review for minors, pregnancy, breastfeeding, chronic disease, prescription medications, severe symptoms, and all higher-risk botanicals or compound formulas.
5. Exclude metallic/mineral preparations and any product without U.S.-grade certificate-of-analysis documentation from consumer recommendations.

## FDA Considerations

FDA materials distinguish food/dietary supplement claim categories, including health claims, nutrient content claims, and structure/function claims. For dietary supplements, structure/function claims require substantiation, the required FDA disclaimer, and FDA notification no later than 30 days after first marketing.

FDA also states that Ayurvedic products marketed to prevent, treat, diagnose, or cure disease are illegally marketed in the U.S., and warns that some Ayurvedic products can contain harmful levels of lead, mercury, arsenic, or hidden drug ingredients. FDA has used warning letters and import alerts to help stop violative products from entering the U.S.

Product implication:

- Store every displayed claim in a claim register.
- Block disease terms from consumer-facing product claims.
- Keep required disclaimers adjacent to structure/function claims when supplements are marketed.
- Require supplier documentation, batch traceability, and contaminant testing.
- Do not treat "traditional use" as enough for a safety or efficacy claim.

## FTC Considerations

FTC health-product advertising guidance says health benefit and safety claims generally require competent and reliable scientific evidence. FTC also applies substantiation requirements to traditional-use claims.

Product implication:

- Maintain an evidence register for every claim.
- Record express and implied claims created by the UI, ads, emails, onboarding, search results, and recommendation language.
- Avoid before/after, cure, disease reversal, or guaranteed outcome claims.
- Make limitations clear without burying them in fine print.

## Safety And Clinical Governance

Build these controls before any real consumer launch:

- Red-flag triage before recommendations.
- Contraindication screening.
- Medication interaction screening.
- Pregnancy, breastfeeding, and pediatric routing.
- Liver/kidney disease routing.
- Allergy and ingredient matching.
- Heavy-metal and contaminant exclusion rules.
- Adverse-event reporting workflow.
- Human reviewer queue for high-risk outputs.
- Versioned knowledge base with source and reviewer signoff.
- Audit trail for every recommendation output.

## India-To-U.S. Export Risks

The export model is possible, but it needs disciplined controls:

- U.S.-compliant dietary supplement labeling.
- Facility and supplier due diligence.
- GMP documentation.
- Product classification review.
- Customs/import counsel review.
- Certificate of analysis for each batch.
- Heavy metal, microbial, pesticide, and adulterant testing.
- Label claim review before sale.
- Import-alert screening for suppliers/products.
- Recall and adverse-event process.

## Privacy And Health Data

Even when HIPAA does not apply, the app should treat symptoms, medications, activity, goals, and practitioner notes as sensitive data.

Minimum product controls:

- Explicit consent for health/activity data.
- Separate consent for Apple HealthKit, Google Fit/Health Connect, Pacer, email, and practitioner sharing.
- Data minimization.
- Export and deletion rights.
- Encryption in transit and at rest.
- Access logs.
- Role-based access for practitioners/admins.
- Clear retention policy.

## Product Positioning Recommendation

Use:

- "Ayurvedic wellness guidance"
- "source-aware lifestyle and botanical education"
- "practitioner-review support"
- "safety-screened intake"
- "supports healthy routines"

Avoid:

- "treatment generator"
- "diagnoses your ailment"
- "prescribes Ayurvedic medicine"
- "cures chronic disease"
- "FDA-approved Ayurveda"
